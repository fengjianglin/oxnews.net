High-tech careers draw growing number of workers as industry upgrades create new jobs High-tech careers draw rising number of workers

2019-04-14InsightTags: BIZ FOCUS, INDUSTRIES, INSIGHT



Zhu Beibei, a Hubei resident, flies a plant protection unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) in Alar, northwest China’s Xinjiang Uygur Autonomous Region on Friday. After trying several “traditional jobs” like pesticide seller and assembly line worker, he chose to become a UAV controller. Photo: Courtesy of Zhu Beibei

As China undergoes industrial upgrades, a new set of job titles has emerged – Internet of Things technicians and installers, big data technicians, unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) controllers, and industrial robot operators – along with an enthusiastic crop of young workers.

Zhu Beibei stood at one corner of a vast field of pear trees in Alar, Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region around noon on Friday. 

In front of him, a black unmanned aerial vehicle hovered several meters above the field, spraying pesticide onto the trees.

Zhu, 29, was born in Huanggang in Central China’s Hubei Province and has been working as a UAV controller for about three years. His team of about 60 people, most of them in their 20s and 30s, has been hired by farmers to spray pesticide onto the fields with the help of UAVs. 

On a normal work day, Zhu gets up at 5 am, prepares and cleans the equipment, and drives with his colleagues to the field at about 7 am. There they measure the size and position of the field and fly the UAVs.

At noon, when the sun is very strong, Zhu eats and rests at the houses of local farmers. He rests until about 5 pm and starts flying the UAVs again until about midnight. After wrapping up work, he gets back home and charges the UAV batteries. At 2 or 3 am, he goes to bed. 

Before working as a UAV controller, Zhu tried several “traditional” jobs such as working at a Wuhan-based car manufacturer and later as a pesticide vendor. 

Compared to these more conventional professions, Zhu said that the UAV controller job is “more suitable for young people.”

“It has much more freedom than an ordinary job,” Zhu told the Global Times on Friday night. Recalling his job as an assembly line worker at the car manufacturer, Zhu said he worked at the factory through the night “like a robot.” He couldn’t talk to others and had no hope of promotion. 

But the UAV controller job is different as it offers freedom and a little spark of excitement, which Zhu likes. As he drives from one province to another on a mission to protect farmers’ crops, he gets to see the scenery change, which he finds thrilling. “When we work, we get a lot of help from local farmers. It makes me feel my job is valued and there’s a kind of interpersonal communication in it,” he said. 

On April 3, UAV controller was listed along with another 12 jobs as new professions in China, according to a statement released by the
Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security on its website.

The other 12 jobs included Internet of Things technicians and installers, big data technicians, agricultural managers, e-sports players, industrial robot operators and cloud computing technicians. 

Ye Hang, an economics professor at the College of Economics at Zhejiang University, said he approved of the release of the new jobs, and noted the government recognition would likely result in more legal protection for employees in the new professions.

Zhu said he was very happy that his job was officially recognized by the government. “In the past, I often introduced myself as a ‘UAV pesticide spray team leader.’ Now I have a formal title,” he said. 

New industries

Zhu’s passion for his UAV controller job mirrors a rising trend among Chinese people, especially the younger generation, who are embracing job opportunities provided by emerging industries.

These jobs offer not only a good salary, but also a kind of excitement that can stoke their interest. 

A report by the Economic Daily on April 5 depicted the job of a 25-year-old Internet of Things installer named Zhang Haiyang, who helps install and test facial recognition payment machines at Huaqiangbei, an electronics market in Shenzhen in South China’s Guangdong Province.

“If you understand the technology and are willing to endure hardships, it’s not difficult to earn 200,000 yuan ($29,840)in one year on the job,” Zhang was quoted by the media as saying. In comparison, an ordinary worker at a Foxconn factory earns only 40,000 yuan each year, according to media reports. 

Another young man from Hubei, 21-year-old Ding Cong, decided to quit his university studies when a semi-professional eSports club invited him to join. After several years of training, he is now a player with a professional eSports club called VG. 

On normal training days, he gets up at 11 am and trains from 2 pm to 9 pm. He sometimes goes to the gym after that for a workout. But during matches, his schedule is much tighter with much more pressure, he said.

“I know outsiders think of us as just playing computer games all the time. But in fact, we are a team fighting for a championship just like traditional sports players. There’s a spiritual feeling about it,” he told the Global Times on Wednesday. 

Behind these young people’s career decisions is the fact that burgeoning new industries, especially with the rapid development of high-tech fields like the internet and intelligent manufacturing in China, have opened up new job markets. 

According to a report by askci.com in June 2018, China’s eSports market value reached about 5.58 billion yuan in 2018, up from 4.06 billion yuan in 2017, and will likely reach 11.6 billion yuan by the end of 2021. 

Statistics revealed by the Tencent eSports division showed that there are about 50,000 people working in the eSports industry today, and there are an additional 200,000 related job vacancies, according to media reports. 

According to Ding, the eSports industry offers a variety of jobs,including data analyst, match host and designers. He said that when he is 25, he might consider becoming a video game host.

The situation is similar in other new industries. The manager of a Shenzhen-based facial recognition machine seller surnamed Su told the Global Times on condition of anonymity that his company is looking for Internet of Things technicians who can do front-end software development to explore application scenarios. 

According to Su, the technical threshold for an Internet of Things technician is much higher than for an Internet of Things installer, and it is very hard to find suitable candidates for the job.

“We want people who have worked for online giants like Tencent and Alibaba,” he said. 

More skilled workers are also being attracted to UAV crop protection. 

Cheng Yi, a UAV instructor at the Xaircraft Academy, a school for training UAV controllers, said that the academy’s branch in Xuzhou, a city in East China’s Jiangsu Province, trains about 300 controllers each year. 

“Some of them are traditional farmers who want to use new farming technologies for their fields. Some of them are ex-soldiers who are looking for jobs, and some are university students. Most of them were born in the 1970s or 1980s,” Cheng told the Global Times. 

A report released by the Qianzhan Industry Research Institute in 2017 noted that the market value of UAV crop protection reached 9.03 billion yuan in 2017 and could reach 10.66 billion yuan by the end of 2020. 

Growing recognition

As these new industries boom in China, related professions are gaining more recognition from customers and society. 

Ding said that when he first decided to work as an eSports player, his parents didn’t support him and didn’t understand his new job. But later, when he won a championship, they started to feel proud of him. 

“Some of my friends say I am cool when they find out I am an eSports player. But they wouldn’t consider doing it. All this job needs is talent,” he said. 

Zhu also noted that when he first started as a UAV controller, nobody supported him. “They thought that UAVs were too high-tech for me to handle,” he said. “But actually, the technical threshold for this job is not very high, and I know someone born in the 1960s who has learned to become a UAV controller,” he said. 

But he also has felt a change of attitude toward his job and the UAV industry over time. “Now my parents have completely accepted this job. Some of my friends have also joined my team after they saw what I am doing,” he said. 

He mentioned that business is improving as crop protection UAVs have gained popularity. “At first, farmers were very curious about UAVs and asked questions such as whether a person could sit on a UAV. Now they are very familiar with the gadgets and wouldn’t pay any special attention to them,” he said. 

More new jobs likely

But along with the emergence of these new jobs, many traditional jobs are losing their appeal or shrinking in scale as a result of industrial upgrades. For example, the emergence of industrial robots creates new jobs, but also cuts the traditional labor force.  

Su said that digital payment tools will cut traditional cashier jobs, so the machines are welcomed by shopkeepers as they can reduce business costs. 

A report by ifeng.com in 2016 noted that industrial upgrading has caused many workers in Dongguan, Guangdong to lose their jobs, and some migrant workers were quoted as saying that they felt hopeless about the future. 

But Ye from Zhejiang University said that this transformation is a kind of social improvement. “It provides a direction for the future. The painful evolution from the old job market to the new one is something that China has to experience. We need to embrace it,” Ye told the Global Times on Tuesday. 

He also noted that industrial upgrading has given rise to many non-technical jobs, such as delivery people, but the government should see to it that their labor rights are also protected.
