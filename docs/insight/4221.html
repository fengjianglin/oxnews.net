Equestrian sports race ahead in Greater Bay Area under new joint development plan Joint development plan to usher in new era for equestrian sports in Greater Bay Area

2019-04-16InsightTags: INSIGHT



A student rider practices pony jumping during an equestrian class in Beijing. Photo: Courtesy of IPony Club

Horse fanciers, jockeys and sports fans in China have reason to rejoice thanks to a partnership between Guangdong Province and the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR) to develop the region’s equine industry.

Conghua Racecourse in Guangdong hosted the first world-class horse racing event on the Chinese mainland on March 23. The race, which was jointly organized by the Hong Kong Jockey Club and local authorities in Conghua, drew the participation and attendance of top officials from throughout the region. 

Top officials pledged to push forward development of world-class professionals in horse-related industries on the Chinese mainland and to ensure the continuing growth of equestrian sports in the region, noting that Hong Kong and Guangdong have created an excellent model for what can be accomplished with the Greater Bay Area development.

The event was the first among many under the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area development plan to advance the region’s equine industry. According to a grand blueprint for the development of the area, which was unveiled in February by the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China and the State Council, China’s cabinet, exchanges between the mainland and Hong Kong in equestrian-related industries will be fully supported and strengthened.

The plan vowed to promote exchanges between the two sides in import and export inspection and quarantine and customs clearance for horses, feed, veterinary drugs and related products.

Under the guideline, there will be more preferential policies to boost communications in such sectors as people-to-people exchanges, the flow of goods and inspection and quarantine services, Luo Dongbin, deputy managing director and secretary-general of Guangdong Equestrian Association, told the Global Times over the weekend. 

Joint development

An official at the Guangdong sports bureau told the Global Times that Guangzhou cross-border customs has simplified quarantine supervision procedures for horses from Hong Kong imported to Conghua Racecourse for training and competition. Horses from Hong Kong designated for the race course need only pass through a disinfection channel for vehicles and an automatic temperature detection channel.  

About 300 horses are imported and exported to and from Conghua Racecourse through Guangzhou customs every week, the Southern Metropolis Daily reported. 

The joint development of the Greater Bay Area will attract more jockeys from Hong Kong and Macao to participate in equestrian sports in the Chinese mainland, according to Luo. “Such competitions provide a platform for deeper cooperation in other upstream and downstream industries, including horse breeding and horse auctions. It will speed up the growth of equine industries in the Chinese mainland.”

Frequent communication with the two special administrative regions will also help mainland industry players establish a database for horses, especially star racehorses, in order to assess the strength of different horses and create training plans accordingly, Luo said.

Guangdong’s opportunities  

To many, the joint development plan signals a golden period ahead for the equestrian industry in Guangdong, which has had a checkered history in past years. 

With its geographic proximity to HKSAR, which has a 130-year tradition of horse-racing, Guangdong should grasp the opportunity to become a pioneer and a leader in equestrian sports on the mainland, analysts said.

In fact, Guangdong, considered the frontier of China’s reform and opening-up, first started developing its horse racing industry 20 years ago. 

In January 1993, the Guangdong Race Course opened. During its first two equestrian competitions, the course gained national fame and grossed revenue of about 1 million yuan through issuing lotteries. Its business model largely drew on that of the Hong Kong Jockey Club, the horse racing operator in Hong Kong whose revenue largely comes from gambling. 

At its peak, races at the course could attract more than 30,000 spectators. But in 1999, the horse racing industry in Guangdong suddenly came to an end as controversy over illegal gambling mounted, which prompted regulators to halt the race course’s operation, industry insiders told the Global Times.

Under current Chinese laws and regulations, which prohibit gambling, Guangdong sports authorities are still struggling to find a path to boost the development of local horse-related sports while abiding the rules. 

“The sound and long-term development of horse-related sports requires complete upstream and downstream support – in other words, the input of capital, resource and talent. But how to gain funds and foster the industry’s ability to generate revenue on its own has been a headache for industry insiders,” Luo said.

Luo said that the first step toward driving industry growth is to elevate awareness of equestrian sports in the region. “We will send more jockeys to participate in international competitions such as the Olympics in the near future.”

“The general public lacks an understanding of equestrian sports,” Luo said. “Some Chinese think the sports are dangerous, while most consider them elite sports and are unable to appreciate equestrian events such as dressage. Most horses in the clubs on [the Chinese mainland] are also not of good quality.” 

Booming demand 

The policy boost also comes amid the industry’s fast development in recent years, though it remains in an early stage. Industry players told the Global Times that with an increase in the country’s economic strength and Chinese’s people’s growing living standards, the general public’s interest and knowledge of equestrian sports have surged in recent years. 

“Previously, compared with most leisure and sports activities, equestrian sports were more expensive and considered unaffordable. Recently, our membership has seen steady growth, showing booming demand backed by strong domestic consumption power,” Lü Jianhui, general manager of Beijing-based Ipony Club, told the Global Times in a recent interview. 

“Teenagers are our biggest growth group by far, and more parents want to take their children to our clubs and register them for classes. We are also cooperating with schools and organizing group classes for students,” Lü said.

The cost of a class is still not cheap. An equestrian class normally lasts 45 minutes and can cost as much as 600 yuan at Lü’s club. “But we’re trying to lower the price for education purposes to increase people’s awareness of the sport.”

Zhang Jie, general manager of the Guangzhou-based equestrian club Honglan, also told the Global Times on Monday that he has seen an increasing number of customers recently and is planning to open a third club in the city at the end of this year thanks to booming demand. 

According to an industry report released in November 2018 by Horsemanship Magazine, there were 1,802 equestrian clubs in the country in 2018, double the total of 907 in 2016. Beijing has 241 clubs, accounting for 12.86 percent of the country’s total and more than anywhere else in China. 

Economic stimulus

The sport will also boost related industries, the development of which will help drive the economy and employment in Guangdong and across the country, observers said. 

Organizing an equestrian club or a race requires a host of related equipment and accessories: bits, bridles, leatherwork, hoods, bibs, fences and stables, to name just a few. And they are not cheap.

On average, a set of hurdles used in a horse race could cost around 150,000 yuan, Pax Huang, general manager of Beijing-based RENHE International Equestrian Equipment Co, told the Global Times on Monday.

Due to the fast development of the industry, the sales volume of his company in 2018 saw a year-on-year jump of 30 percent, Huang said, noting that more companies might come to grab a share of the market in the future.

Travel agencies could also offer horse-related activities to attract more tourists and create new sources of revenue, Luo noted.

Industry players called for more preferential policies under the general guideline of the Greater Bay Area, such as land lease, media exposure, industry subsidies as well as a unified industry regulation in the near future.

Showing his optimism about the industry, Huang, who has been in the industry for 16 years, said he is still looking forward to a policy release in the sports lottery business some day in the future, which will unleash more potential, attract more participants, and usher in a “surging period.” 

“But China still needs some time to improve its management systems, including race supervision and jockey training, before access to horse racing and a sports lottery could be eased,”Huang said.
