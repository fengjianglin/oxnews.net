Rural designer, misunderstood by fellow villagers, learns acceptance while clinging to her vision

2018-07-19ProfileTags: PROFILE



Iconoclastic designer Wang Shouying Photo: Courtesy of Wang Shouying

On a typical blind date, Wang Shouying usually spends 10 to 20 minutes talking with the man on the street, judging whether he is her Mr. Right.

The village woman, who rose to fame a few years ago on social media for her iconoclastic clothing design and thus grew ambitious to change her fate by becoming an international designer, has been tamed by the realities of rural life.

Under the pressure from her parents and peers to get married and have children, the path that every typical rural woman is expected to follow, Wang has been busy with blind dating. So far, she has been on about 100 such blind dates. However, she is still single. In her eyes, 100 dates is not a huge number for the “leftover men and women” at her age, the 29-year-old rural fashion designer said.

During Wang’s recent interview with the Global Times, the phrase she uttered most frequently was “let it be.” She applied this sentiment not only to her prospects for marriage, but also her clothing design career.

Wang was popular on Chinese TV shows and social media in 2013 and 2014, which encouraged her to dream big. But since then, Wang said she has made little progress in her design career and is meanwhile “falling behind” rural girls of her age in entering a marriage and having children.

It seems her vision and confidence play well on a national stage, but are not appreciated in her village.

‘Clothes are concepts’

Wang used to be a farmer in Nanliuquan village, a two-hour drive from the city of Tai’an in Shandong Province. She dreamed she would become a successful designer after her eye-catching works have been featured on popular reality shows, such as “The Brilliant Chinese,” during which the famous Chinese actress Fan Bingbing promised to take her to Paris Fashion Week after she saw Wang’s design.

However, every time when she came back from TV shows or a garment factory, she woke up with empty dreams. “From 2014 till now, everybody kept telling me how they could help me. But after I took part in shows or cooperated with merchants, there were few results,” said Wang. She now works as a part-time event organizer at a local culture-related company.

Wang said being on reality TV shows brought her many problems.

At the very beginning, there were garment companies and business shows coming to her for cooperation. She turned them down, because she thought making clothes that could be commercially successful was too tiring.

Wang, like many other designers, believes, “Clothes are concepts, rather than for being sold on the market.”

“There were business agents coming to me for cooperation, but after I found they are not reliable, I switched to filming shows, since they told me I could display my clothes,” she said. But she added this was a wrong decision, again. She feels that she missed out on “the best period of time” for her to promote her clothes.

It can be seen from news reporting and Chinese social media that many people call her a web celebrity who comes from countryside and makes bizarre clothes in order to generate publicity for herself.

Wang strongly rejects the label “wanghong” (online celebrity). Instead, she prefers to be called a designer.



One of Wang’s high concept designs, in which she uses a thread connecting food steamers as a skirt Photo: Courtesy of Wang Shouying

A hundred blind dates

Some men and their families who have conducted blind dates with Wang thought she was “not obedient.” Some think she is rich. Others think she may have a mental disorder. For her, being a famous clothes designer has made her quest to find true love more difficult.

Girls in her village start going on blind dates quite early, since most of them stop education in primary school. Wang went for her first blind date set up by a matchmaker when she was 17.  Most village women find a husband early. Starting in 2015, her family arranged blind dates more frequently for Wang. Sometimes she would meet three or four men within one day. In order to record her matchmaking experiences, Wang is keeping a blind date diary.

“Once, a man asked me to pay the bill of our meal, because he said I am much wealthier than him. After I paid the bill, he requested that I transfer him money through our mobile phones, because he thought I am a web celebrity who must be rich,” she said with laughter.

A fitness instructor she dated asked her to help advertise his classes after he found out Wang has been on TV shows.

Even though some men do not come for love but for her fame, Wang said she doesn’t reject matchmaking. She just doesn’t put too much hope in it these days. “I used to suffer insomnia for several days before blind dates, and got very excited on the day, but now all those feelings are gone,” she said.



One of Wang Shouying’s designs decorated with bottle caps Photo: Courtesy of Wang Shouying

Still designing

Wang now sometimes makes live videos on Weibo to show her design ideas and latest designed clothes.

Recalling her previous dream of establishing her own clothing brand, she calls herself too stubborn. Being tired of strange looks from villagers, she avoids walking in public wearing clothes the villagers can’t understand.

She does not dare to move to a big city, fearing that she could not make ends meet.

Wang said she no longer has career goals. Like her philosophy toward marriage, her attitude toward life is “let it be.”

“I don’t think a women’s career must be crucially vital, and I also don’t agree that marriage is the most important thing for girls.” She notes some young women just get married according to a schedule set by society. “That’s too limited. They don’t even have freedom,” Wang told the Global Times.

In the past, she hoped her clothes would look good on other people and win recognition, but now, she cares more about whether the clothes she wears are comfortable or not, whether she thinks they look interesting or not, and whether the clothes on her could be seen as exotic to other people.

“My love for clothes cannot be given up right away. It has already been part of my life,” she said. “Unless I lose my memory, I will love clothing design forever.”
