Stand-up comic risks everything just to raise a few smiles

2017-06-15ProfileTags: PROFILE



Zhong Yongming performs stand-up comedy outside a subway station in Guangzhou, Guangdong Province. Photo: Yang Jingru/The Beijing News

Zhong Yongming is probably China’s only full-time stand-up comedy street performer.

He quit his well paid job to pursue his childhood dream of being an actor and making people laugh, but despite spending eight years practicing and teaching himself stand-up comedy, few people understand his art or see the funny side. Even his brother said he lives more like a beggar.

“Do you know why men don’t like women with broad faces? Because a woman’s face is the most expensive thing in the world to decorate!”

Outside Chenjiaci subway station in Guangzhou, South China’s Guangdong province, 38-year-old Zhong stands on the steps, waving his hands and making baffled, startled or teasing facial expressions to go with his jokes – his money-collection box, loudspeaker and bottle of water lie beside him.

He is thin and sun-tanned, his long hair just reaches his shoulders, and his eyes behind a pair of glasses stare straight ahead. He usually wears a T-shirt printed with Chinese characters reading Zhong Yongming Stand-up Comedy when performing on the street, in Putonghua, Cantonese and some English.

It is evening rush hour, and throngs of commuters pass through the subway station every few minutes, but few people pay any attention to Zhong’s performance. In two hours, only two groups of people stop to listen – three men handing out fliers outside the station and two children in middle school uniforms. A food deliveryman on his scooter stops for a few seconds while waiting for the traffic lights, and murmurs “Not comparable to Huang Zihua.”

Huang is an actor in Hong Kong who first introduced the genre, which originated in the US, to Hong Kong in the 1990s. In 2009, Zhong saw Huang on TV and was instantly struck by his performance.

It was from that moment that Zhong decided to master the art. “You can make hundreds or thousands of people laugh. How wonderful it must feel!”

He did thorough research into stand-up comedy by watching performance clips of all the top 100 American comedians. For two years, he spent all his spare time watching stand-up comedy shows, fantasizing about being the performer at the center of the stage and thinking stand-up comedy was his destiny.

At that time, he was working as a translator in a molding factory. It was a job hard-earned by Zhong.

Zhong started his career in the factory as a molding apprentice in 1998 after graduating from high school. After years of dull machine work, one day he was struck by a desire to learn English.

Zhong’s village home is 40 kilometers from the city of Guangzhou. Every morning before 5 am, Zhong learned English by reciting what he heard on the radio. In the first two years he could hardly understand anything he recited, but by 2005, he was able to comprehend more than 80 percent of an English story.

In 2005, he was given the opportunity to accompany an American visitor for a month, and surprisingly found he was good enough to work as an interpreter. He then became the factory translator in charge of overseas communications, and his pay jumped from just over 1,000 yuan ($147) to 6,000 yuan a month.

But after 2009, when he became addicted to stand-up comedy, he found it harder to do his work. Not one to take the easy option, Zhong was forced to make a choice between security and following his dream. Against fierce opposition from his elderly father, he quit his job during the Spring Festival in 2011 to pursue stand-up comedy. He was 32 at the time.

Zhong fell back on his self-taught knowledge of stand-up comedy. During his years of study, he had written 234 pages of jokes and self rebuttals, amounting to 130,000 words.

He began performing on the street in July 2013. That day, standing at the entrance to the Baiyuanshan scenic spot, he was so nervous he couldn’t say a word for 40 minutes. Eventually he started to talk and managed to keep it up for half an hour but only caught the attention of a toddler.

Zhong keeps notes of every performance and the money he receives. Since June 2015 until now, he has performed 110 times and received 598 yuan in total. He did not receive a penny for 60 of the performances, and the largest contribution was 100 yuan from an American.

Just before the Moon Festival in 2015, he invited 20 friends and former classmates to a show at his home. Only four people showed up and none laughed during the one and a half hour performance.

That made Zhong regret organizing the show.

He believes his performance was fine, and it was the audience that was unable to get his humor.

Without income, Zhong had to move back to live with his parents and two younger brothers, who have both married and had children.

His brother Zhong Shaoming has helped him financially, but says “I won’t help him much if he continues to be like this.”

Zhong Shaoming thinks his brother should try to understand what people are really interested in. “Stand-up comedy is a Western concept. Here we have a different culture, you can’t copy others’ stuff.”

But Zhong Yongming insists art is “free and limitless.” “Audiences can be cultivated, and I believe I will succeed one day,” he says.

Now he goes to Guangzhou every morning. In order to save the 17 yuan bus fare, he rides his electric scooter to a small town and takes a slow bus to the nearest subway station, and then rides the subway to the city center.

He has performed at all major subway stations. In order to attract as many people as possible, he posted a QR code of his WeChat account on the loudspeaker.

He now has 163 “fans” in the WeChat group, though most of them are advertisers.
